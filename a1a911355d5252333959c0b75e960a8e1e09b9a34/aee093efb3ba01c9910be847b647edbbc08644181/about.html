A Composite Biodiversity Index

Biodiversity indexes are mathematical measures used by ecologists to quantify the variety of life in an ecosystem.

Here we develop a new approach to calculate three biodiversity indexes that will be combined as the Multidimensional Biodiversity Index (MBI) at the end.

A composite biodiversity index is a multi-layered metric designed to aggregate diverse datasets—often spanning different taxa, ecosystems, or ecological dimensions—into a single summary trend to aid decision-makers and communicate complex biological changes.

Because biodiversity is a broad concept encompassing species, genetic, and ecosystem diversity, it is difficult to capture holistically in any single indicator.

Purpose and Features of a Robust Composite Index

The primary goal of a composite index is to determine the rate of biodiversity change over time for specific regions or species groups.

A high-quality biodiversity index should meet several technical criteria:

  • Sensitivity to Key Components: It should decrease if overall abundance, species richness, or species evenness (the similarity in abundance among different species) decreases.

  • Independence from Sample Size: The expected value should not be a function of the sample size.

  • Precision: It should have a measurable and reliable level of precision.

  • Handling Dominance: It should ideally reduce the influence of a single dominant species to prevent it from masking trends on rarer species.

NoteWhat Should a Good Biodiversity Indicator Look Like?

Measure diversity at various levels:

  • Taxonomic diversity (not just species richness)
  • Functional diversity (the “jobs” that species do)
  • Structural diversity (habitat complexity)

Technical Formulations and Dimensions

A robust composite biodiversity indicator may integrate information across different ecological levels. Here we propose a conceptual formulation. The Multidimensional Biodiversity Index (MBI), combines three primary dimensions:

Taxonomic Diversity (TD): Accounts for species richness and relative abundances, often using the geometric mean of abundances to ensure proportional sensitivity.

Functional Diversity (FD): Measures the diversity of ecological roles or “jobs” species perform, which is linked to ecosystem resilience and services.

Structural Diversity (SD): Reflects physical habitat complexity, such as canopy height variation in forests, often quantified via LiDAR.

Taxonomic Diversity (geometric mean of occupancy)

\[ TD_{t}=exp(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i}^{} ln \frac{d_{i,t}}{d_{i,1}}) \] were: - n = number of species - di,t = abundance of species i at time t - di,1 = abundance of species i at time 1

Functional Diversity (traits/jobs)

Rooted at 1 which allows to evaluate changes over time Can be generalized to n traits (continuous and discrete)

\[ FD_{t}=\frac{\sum_{i}^{}z_{i,t}a_{i,t}}{\sum_{i}^{}z_{i,1}a_{i,1}} \] were: - zi,t = distance to centroid for species i at time t - ai,1 = relative abundance of species i at time 1 (also occupancy)

Structural Diversity (Foliage height diversity)

Rooted at 1 which allows to evaluate changes over time

\[ SD_{t}=-\sum_{i=1}^{n}f_{i,t}ln f_{i,t}/SD_{1} \] were: - SD = Foliage height diversity at time t - fi,t = proportion of foliage at heigh at time t

Multidimensional Biodiversity Index (MBI)

\[ MBI_{t}=exp(\frac{ln TD_{t} + ln FD_{t} + ln SD_{t} }{3}) \]

Notewere the results of MIB indicate:
  • MBI > 0 Increasing.
  • MBI < 0 Decreasing.
  • MBI = 0 Unchanged.
